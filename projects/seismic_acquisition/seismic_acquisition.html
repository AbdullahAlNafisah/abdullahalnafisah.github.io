Wireless Seismic Data Acquisition System

Best Senior Project Award, Senior Expo Day, KFUPM, 2019

Overview

This project presents a wireless seismic data acquisition system designed for geophysical surveys. Traditional surveys deploy thousands of cabled sensors, which are costly and cumbersome; here, we replace them with compact wireless nodes built around coil-based geophone sensors. Each node integrates a low-noise signal-conditioning chain (programmable gain and anti-aliasing filter), a 24-bit sigma-delta ADC sampled every 2 ms, and a microcontroller (ATmega328P on Arduino Nano) that configures the converter and handles framing. Data packets are transmitted over a Zigbee link (XBee S2C), enabling distributed deployment without long analog cables. This architecture provides high resolution, low power, and robust digital transport suitable for low-frequency seismic acquisition.

Sensor Model

The analog front-end uses a geophone to capture ground motion: a stationary magnet and a moving coil produce an induced voltage proportional to relative velocity by Faraday’s Law.

\[ v_g(t) \propto \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \]

Modeling the mechanics as a damped harmonic oscillator with coil displacement \(x(t)\) and ground displacement \(u(t)\):

\[ \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + 2\lambda \omega_0 \frac{dx}{dt} + \omega_0^2 x = \frac{d^2u}{dt^2} \]

The transfer functions for displacement-, velocity-, and acceleration-proportional outputs are:

\[ H_{\text{displacement}}(s) = A \cdot \frac{s^3}{s^2 + 2\lambda \omega_0 s + \omega_0^2} \] \[ H_{\text{velocity}}(s) = A \cdot \frac{s^2}{s^2 + 2\lambda \omega_0 s + \omega_0^2} \] \[ H_{\text{acceleration}}(s) = A \cdot \frac{s}{s^2 + 2\lambda \omega_0 s + \omega_0^2} \]

The velocity transfer function’s equal numerator/denominator orders yield a flat high-frequency response, so the sensor effectively acts as a velocity transducer for seismic signals.

Signal Conditioning & 24-Bit Σ-Δ ADC

The analog signal is digitized by the AD7714: a 24-bit sigma-delta ADC with integrated PGA and digital filtering, low standby power (~15 μW), and fully differential inputs. The Arduino Nano configures the ADC over SPI (register setup, gain, rate, and self-calibration) and relays framed samples to the Zigbee radio. For this prototype, we used a 2 ms sampling interval (500 Hz), differential input mapping (AIN1–AIN6), and startup self-calibration.

Verification Results

A hammer impulse was applied and captured by both a benchmark system and this prototype. Results show successful acquisition and reliable wireless transmission up to 50 m.
