Faster-Than-Nyquist (FTN)

Communication systems transmit and receive data either directly using the source baseband signal or by modulating and demodulating a passband carrier signal at higher frequencies. Essentially, this depends on the application, the channel, and the available bandwidth. To avoid aliasing in the case of a baseband signal with bandwidth \(B\), the sampling rate \(f_s\) must satisfies \[ 2B < f_s \] This is known as the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, and the value of \(2B\) is called the Nyquist rate, not to be confused with the Nyquist frequency, which equals \(f_s/2\). The Nyquist rate specifies the minimum sampling rate required to reconstruct the signal without loss of information at the receiver.

In digital communication, the sampling rate should be as high as possible to increase the information throughput. However, communication channels have a finite bandwidth and are susceptible to intersymbol interference (ISI). To eliminate ISI, the signal passes through a pulse shaping filter and a matched filter before and after transmission, respectively. Combined with the channel, the net response \(h(t)\) must satisfy the Nyquist ISI criterion, which is stated here in time and frequency domains \[ \begin{aligned} &\text{Time domain (}T_s=\frac{1}{f_s}\text{):} & h(nT_s) = \begin{cases} 1, & n = 0 \\ 0, & n \neq 0 \end{cases} \\[1em] &\text{Frequency domain:} & f_s \sum_{k=-\infty}^{+\infty} H\left(f - k f_s \right) &= 1, \quad \forall f \end{aligned} \] The raised-cosine filter is typically used to satisfy this condition, where the actual response is limited to the channel bandwidth and divided between the transmit and receive filters by means of two root-raised-cosine filters.
