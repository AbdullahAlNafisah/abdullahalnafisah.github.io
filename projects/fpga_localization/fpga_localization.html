Real-Time FPGA-Based Indoor Localization

This project implements low-cost, real-time indoor localization using precise time-of-flight (ToF) of acoustic pulses, synchronized FPGAs, and hardware-level processing. A master FPGA wirelessly synchronizes two slave FPGAs (transmitter/receiver). The transmitter emits an ultrasonic waveform based on a Zadoff-Chu (ZC) sequence, while the receiver performs ADC sampling, demodulation, filtering, and correlation to estimate ToF with 100 MHz timing resolution. With an analog front-end (BPF + gain), the system achieves stable ranging with sub-centimeter MAE and synchronization jitter within ±1.2 µs.

Master FPGA Module

A wireless trigger (NRF24L01) simultaneously starts the transmitter and receiver time bases (100 MHz clock), enabling accurate ToF measurement between emission and detection.

Acoustic Transmitter

ZC sequence (N=16, root=1) is modulated on a 40 kHz carrier and sent to an ultrasonic transducer. Typical parameters: upsampling = 256, window = 4096, sampling = 320 ksps.

Acoustic Receiver & AFE

The receiver demodulates I/Q, low-pass filters, then cross-correlates with the stored ZC reference to detect arrival time. The analog chain uses a 40.6 kHz-centered BPF (BW≈24 kHz) and amplification before digitization.

Experimental Setup & Results

Tests on Nexys A7 & Basys3 with NRF24L01 synchronization and ultrasonic transducers showed consistent range estimates; timing jitter was within ±1.2 µs and MAE < 1 cm.
